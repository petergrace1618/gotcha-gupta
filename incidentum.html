Incidentum

A detailed account of the incident and the events leading up to it can be found here.

Synopsis

I was tired of the roller coaster that our relationship had become (the last straw was when she gaslighted me about the rent in September 2024), so I contacted an old friend.

Leslie said she happened to have a room open, and I could move in if I wanted. I told her that I would have to clean her place before I moved in because it was filthy. I came over several times in October to do so.

Meanwhile, I was still on the fence about moving out. I wanted to make sure that I was making the right decision for all parties concerned which were G, me, our roommate Adam, and Leslie.

The night of the incident (Saturday November 2 2024) I went to Leslie’s again to clean.

Timeline

  • I arrive at Leslie’s around 7:20 PM and meet her friend Matty for the first time.
  • G calls me after I arrive. I tell her that I’m going to spend the night at Leslie’s and will be back tomorrow.
  • A couple hours later, she calls me again and threatens to throw my stuff out. I tell her more than once not to do it.
  • I call our roommate, Adam, to tell him she’s threatening to throw my stuff out, and ask if he would go to the apartment to make sure she doesn’t.
  • A few minutes later, Adam sends me an angry text message saying he went there and she wasn’t throwing my stuff out.
  • I call the Portland Police Non-Emergency number, and explain that my girlfriend is threatening to throw my stuff out.
  • G sends me two text messages: one asking if she should remove my belongings from the apartment, and the other stating that someone suggested that she do so.
  • An hour and a half later, she sends me a text message that reads "Hey, your friend came over and I gave em some of your stuff."
  • One minute later, she sends another text message stating that she’d been trying to confirm, but I wouldn’t answer my phone, so she guesses that “it’s all good.”
  • She leaves a voicemail where she claims that my friend is there asking for everything. She again admits that she hasn’t received any confirmation from me, but she’s going to give him my stuff anyway.
  • She sends another text message stating that because she hasn’t heard from me, she’s not giving anymore of my stuff away. She then says “This entire thing seems shady af”
  • Her last text message reads “They say they’re picking it up for you, but you’re not answering my phone calls. So I’m not giving it to them.”

[ Source]

I interpreted her last text message to mean that she was bluffing and didn’t actually give my stuff away; however, two days later when I moved out, the aforementioned belongings were missing.

Analysis

Legally speaking, one could feasibly assert that by not responding, my silence implied consent because:

However, this is a sham defense by definition:

And it’s a sham defense for the following reasons:

One, it’s false in fact because I didn’t send anyone to the apartment to pick up any of my belongings. I stated this in my Declaration which was made under penalty of perjury and which I will repeat here.

The following evidence corroborates this:

  • Matty's witness testimony
  • My call and text message history of that weekend

Two, it was not made in good faith because several hours before she claimed that she gave away some of my stuff to my “friend”,

  1. She expressed her intent to remove my belongings from the apartment;
  2. She said that someone suggested that she do so; and
  3. I told her more than once over the phone not to do it.

The following evidence corroborates this:

  • Matty's witness testimony
  • My call to Non-Emergency dispatch
  • The text message from our roommate Adam
  • Her own text messages from that night

Furthermore, her own language in her text messages and voicemail on the night of the incident is unequivocal regarding who did what.

  • “Hey, your friend came over and I gave em some of your stuff.”
  • “Am I supposed to give him this stuff”
  • “Do I have to give your friend everything?”
  • “You’re not answering So I’m not giving anymore of your stuff until you do.”
  • “They say they’re picking it up for you, but you’re not answering my phone calls. So I’m not giving it to them.”

Conclusion

It’s clear that she was the actor, and the one responsible for giving my stuff away.

At no point did she say “Your friend came over and took your stuff,” or anything substantially equivalent. Nor did she use any language indicating that they barged in, broke in, climbed in through the window, or otherwise entered forcibly.

Even the phrase “they’re picking it up for you” implies a handing over and acceptance, rather than an appropriation; and just the fact that she used the singular tense and then switched to the plural is suspicious in itself.

In any case, “by the preponderance of evidence” is the standard of proof in a civil suit which means it’s more likely than not that the defendant did what the plaintiff alleges.

Given the aforestated facts, I think any reasonable and rational person would conclude, by the preponderance of evidence, that it’s far more likely than not that she did what she already said she did.

Facts aside

Even if I intended on moving out that weekend (which I didn’t), and I sent people to the apartment to pick up some of my stuff (which I didn’t), I would have let her know. I would have told her who it was, when they were going to be there, and what they were going to pick up, because that would have been the considerate and reasonable thing to do.

Also, if I sent people to the apartment to pick up some of my stuff, it would have been for the purpose of them bringing it to me in which case I would still be in possession of my belongings and would have had no reason to sue her in small claims court.

Also, if I sent people to the apartment to pick up some of my stuff, then there would likely be a record of this in my call and text message history of that night. However there’s no record of this because it never happened. She or anyone else can subpoena Google, Yahoo, Facebook, Instagram, Discord, Slack, Reverbnation, Metal-Archives, Discogs, Telegram, LinkedIn, X, MySpace, Friendster, or f***ing AOL and discover the same thing.

Lastly, if I were in her position and some complete strangers showed up at the apartment claiming that they were her friends and were there to pick up some of her stuff, I would have contacted her to confirm. If I couldn’t get a hold of her, I would have told the people to get her on the phone so that I could confirm with her myself. If they were unwilling or unable to do that, I would have said “Nope. Not happening,” and shut the door in their faces. I don’t care how rocky our relationship was at the time, I wouldn’t let complete strangers who show up in the middle of the night claiming to be her friends into the apartment to take her belongings without any confirmation from her.

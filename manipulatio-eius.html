Manipulatio Eius

Quod Est?

Saturday November 9 2024, a week after "the incident", I came to the apartment to pick up the last of my belongings. While I was packing a large plastic tote on the kitchen counter, she came out of the bedroom and we had the following interaction:

  • “Can you do that tomorrow?”
  • “I’m almost done.”
  • “So come back tomorrow then.”
  • “I’m almost done.”
  • “Then come back tomorrow!”

Her logic nearly baffled me, but I continued packing while she went to the bathroom.

When I finished packing, I put the tote on the porch, and left the front door open so that before I left, I could give her a letter that I had written for her on the way there.

When she came out of the bathroom, I tried to hand it to her. She refused to take it. I insisted. She still refused. I remember her exclaiming “I don’t want it!” so I set it on the shelf just inside the bedroom door.

Not only did she refuse to take it, she refused to even have it in her vicinity. She took the letter and put it in the tote that I had just put on the porch. I told her that if she wouldn’t accept it, I would just tell her what it said.

I don’t have the letter anymore, but I remember the first sentence which I recited to her.

“I was hoping for an amicable break up but your actions precluded it.”

I then explained that what she did was called conversion which is a tort, and the only legal remedy is the return of my belongings. She had no idea what I was talking about, so I made it more concrete:

I told her I wanted my stuff back. She said she didn’t know where it was.

I said, “You gave it away. You can get it back.”

She stomped her foot like a toddler having a tantrum, and yelled “No, I didn’t!”

—

There is a fundamental principle of logic called the Law of Non-Contradiction which states that a proposition and its opposite cannot both be true.

For example, it cannot both be true that she gave away my stuff, and did not give away my stuff.

Regardless of whether she did or didn't, one of those statements must be false; yet, she made both statements, and tried to convince me that they were true.

So again, quod est?

In other words, did she or didn't she?

We may never know.

The Poopyhead Maneuver

I was the recipient of this so often that I gave it a name.

When any criticism or accusation comes her way, whether express or implied, she immediately redirects it back to the person making it–in this case, me. I call it “The Poopyhead Maneuver” because it reminds me of two elementary school kids on the playground. One says to the other “You’re a poopyhead,” and the other says “No, YOU’RE a poopyhead!” And sadly that’s about the level of intelligence required to execute the maneuver.

It’s a brilliant form of manipulation because most normal people when accused or criticized will consider it and address it accordingly. I think she knows this on some level, and that’s why she does it. For one, it puts the other person on the defensive. And two, it allows her to avoid confronting or addressing the accusation or criticism all together. In fact, by doing so she doesn’t have to even acknowledge it. She just immediately and automatically redirects it as if by reflex.

Examples:
No, YOU’RE being unreasonable!
I filed a police report, too!

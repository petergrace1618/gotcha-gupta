Praefatio

Gyps Amanda Melissa Ruiz (hereinafter G) and I lived together from March 2023 to November 2024.

In late August 2024, she went on a trip out of state, and came back broke and unemployed; I paid her rent and bills in September and October.

On November 2 2024, she said in several text messages and a voicemail that she gave away some of my stuff.

On Monday November 4 when I moved out, the following things were missing:

  • Three of my guitars
  • My guitar amp
  • My comic book collection
  • Two guitar stands
  • A collection of about 60 Crown Royal bags, and
  • A bag of kitchen utensils

A week later she denied it.

Six weeks after that I found out she slandered me, so I decided to sue her in small claims court.

DISCLAIMER

This is not defamation, nor doxing, but counterspeech.

All statements I've made about her are true and factual to the best of my knowledge.

All personal information contained herein have been redacted; all else is in the public record except those made by a third party which I presume to be true but may be considered hearsay.

If she wants to tell lies about me, then I'll tell the truth about her.

Some fundamentals first.

Postulate
People generally tell the truth, and assume that others generally tell the truth.
Corollary
If a charming and charismatic girl tells a horror story about her psycho abusive ex-boyfriend, people will most likely believe it.
Caveat
Unless presented with evidence to the contrary.

Well, here's some evidence to the contrary.

Analysis

Legally speaking, one could feasibly assert that by not responding, my silence implied consent because:

One, it’s false in fact because I didn’t send anyone to the apartment to pick up any of my belongings. I stated this in my Declaration which was made under penalty of perjury and which I will repeat here.

Furthermore, her own language in her text messages and voicemail on the night of the incident is unequivocal regarding who did what.

  • “Hey, your friend came over and I gave em some of your stuff.”
  • “Am I supposed to give him this stuff”
  • “Do I have to give your friend everything?”

  • “They say they’re picking it up for you, but you’re not answering my phone calls. So I’m not giving it to them.”

It’s clear that she was the actor, and the one responsible for giving my stuff away. At no point did she say “Your friend came over and took your stuff,” or anything substantially equivalent. Nor did she use any language indicating that they barged in, broke in, climbed in through the window, or otherwise entered forcibly. Even the phrase “they’re picking it up for you” implies a handing over and acceptance, rather than an appropriation.

In a civil case, which includes small claims, the standard of proof is “by the preponderance of evidence” which means, as a lawyer explained to me, it’s more likely than not that the defendant did what the plaintiff alleges.

I think any reasonable person would conclude that, by the preponderance of evidence, it’s far more likely than not that she gave away my stuff as she already stated she did.

Facts aside

Even if I intended on moving out that weekend (which I didn’t), and I sent people to the apartment to pick up some of my stuff (which I didn’t), I would have let her know. I would have told her who it was, when they were going to be there, and what they were going to pick up, because that would have been the considerate and reasonable thing to do.

Also, if I sent people to the apartment to pick up some of my stuff, it would have been for the purpose of them bringing it to me in which case I would still be in possession of my belongings and would have had no reason to sue her in small claims court.

Also, if I sent people to the apartment to pick up some of my stuff, then there would likely be a record of this in my call and text message history of that night. However there’s no record of this because it never happened. She or anyone else can subpoena Google, Yahoo, Facebook, Instagram, Discord, Slack, Reverbnation, Metal-Archives, Discogs, Telegram, LinkedIn, X, MySpace, Friendster, or f***ing AOL and discover the same thing.

Lastly, if I were in her position and some complete strangers showed up at the apartment claiming that they were her friends and were there to pick up some of her stuff, I would have contacted her to confirm. If I couldn’t get a hold of her, I would have told the people to get her on the phone so that I could confirm with her myself. If they were unwilling or unable to do that, I would have said “Nope. Not happening,” and shut the door in their faces. I don’t care how rocky our relationship was at the time, I wouldn’t let complete strangers who show up in the middle of the night claiming to be her friends into the apartment to take her belongings without any confirmation from her.

Initium Mendaciorum

After the incident and her subsequent slander, it occurred to me that if she did that to me, she may have done something similar to her ex-boyfriend (hereinafter E), and fled Utah to escape accountability. To satisfy my morbid curiosity, I contacted one of his family members—a cousin who I will refer to as M—a lovely woman who grew up very close to E who was, in her own words, "like my little brother".

She did not, however, give me permission to publish our email exchange, so I'll summarize her account of their parting which turned out to be a completely different story than the one G related to me.

To demonstrate the contrast, here is...

When she got to Portland, she stayed on a female friend's couch somewhere on Interstate Ave. She got hired at the Alibi soon after. Obviously, the two versions are vastly different. One is purportedly a first hand account while the other is second hand. But which one is more accurate?

Her presence caused tension with his roommate.

She blamed him & punished him because things weren't going well for her.

In September 2023, she got fired from the Alibi for drinking on the job. She felt she was wrongfully terminated even though she had been warned and had been on probation for a number of weeks. Given this corroborating evidence and G's demonstrated mendacity, it seems pretty clear which version is more accurate.

Conclusio

Having related all that unflattering information about her, to balance the scales, I should add in all fairness that I AM NOT PERFECT.

I experience sub-optimum mental phenomena on a regular basis with its attendant physiological manifestations. In other words, I’ve developed a tick. Some of my co-workers who’ve inadvertently witnessed it probably think I have mental problems. But who doesn’t? I’m organized and conscientious, so fuck ‘em.

Also, I have a temper, bad breath, and I fart a lot. Kudos to her for putting up with me. But just because I have a temper doesn't mean I'm violent. If I were, my criminal record would reflect that.

Also, the first seven months G and I were together were amazing. We were madly in love. We spent every day together with few exceptions. She was my best friend. We had a lot of good times and a lot of laughs–even up to the end.

I will also add that I learned a lesson.

I saw a red flag seven months in but I chose to ignore it because I didn't want my rent to go up and I had also grown accustomed to having sex on a regular basis and didn't want to give that up.

(Incidentally, I received more than one I-told-you-so from friends and co-workers after the fact; in hindsight, all I can say is that my reason was eclipsed by her beauty.)

The lesson I learned is when you see a red flag either run, confront it, or break it off. However, if I had broken it off at the time, I wouldn't know what I know now which is that it appears she has a pattern of being an abusive, manipulative, vindictive liar.

But that's just my opinion.

Despite this, after a year and a half of anguish, processing, and reflection, I forgive her.

Does that mean I get my stuff back? We'll see.

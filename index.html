In tort law, implied consent is a defense to an intentional tort. The plaintiff’s consent is implied when the plaintiff fails to object, or is silent in a situation in which a reasonable person would object to the defendant’s action.

– Cornell Law School website, https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/implied_consent ,

Nerd Alert:

There is a fundamental principle of logic called the Law of Non-Contradiction  which states that a proposition and its opposite cannot both be true. For example, it cannot both be true that she gave away my stuff and did not give away my stuff. Regardless of whether she did or didn't, one of those statements must be false. Yet, she made both statements–one repeatedly, the other adamantly–and she tried to convince me that they were true. In other words, she made a false statement with intent to deceive which means by definition she lied.

Considering the circumstances which are that

  • On the afternoon of Saturday November 2, I was in possession of all my belongings;
  • On the evening of Saturday November 2, she said she gave away some of my stuff; and
  • On Monday November 4, some of my stuff was missing;

It seems far more reasonable to conclude that she was telling the truth when she said she gave away my stuff, and was lying when she later said she didn’t.

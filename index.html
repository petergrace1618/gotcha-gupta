I interpreted her last text to mean that she was bluffing and didn’t actually give my stuff away. However, two days later, Monday November 4, when I moved out, the aforementioned belongings were missing.

Analysis

Legally speaking,

In tort law, implied consent is a defense to an intentional tort. The plaintiff's consent is implied when the plaintiff fails to object, or is silent in a situation in which a reasonable person would object to the defendant’s action.
— Cornell Law School website, https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/implied_consent

One could feasibly assert that by not responding, my silence implied consent. However, this is a sham defense by definition,

“A sham plea is good on its face, but false in fact; it may, to all appearances, constitute a perfect defense, but is a pretense because false and because not pleaded in good faith.”
— Black’s Law Dictionary, 4th Edition, pg. 796

And it’s a sham defense for the following reasons.

One, it’s false in fact because I didn’t send anyone to the apartment to pick up any of my stuff. I stated this in my Declaration which was made under penalty of perjury and which I will repeat here.

I did not communicate with anyone via any medium at any time that night or that day or prior to that night or that day for the purpose of sending someone to the apartment to pick up any of my belongings.

The following evidence corroborates this:

  • Declaration of Matthew Rebello
  • Incident Calls and Messages

Two, it was not made in good faith because several hours before she claimed that she gave away some of my stuff to my “friend”

  1. She expressed her intent to remove my belongings from the apartment,
  2. She said that someone suggested that she do so, and
  3. I told her more than once over the phone not to do it.

The following evidence corroborates this:

  • Declaration of Matthew Rebello
  • My conversation with the Non-Emergency dispatcher
  • The text message from our roommate, Adam
  • Her own text messages from that night

Furthermore, her own language in her text messages and voicemail on the night of the incident is unequivocal regarding who did what.

  • “Hey, your friend came over and I gave em some of your stuff.”
  • “Am I supposed to give him this stuff”
  • “Do I have to give your friend everything?”
  • “You’re not answering So I’m not giving anymore of your stuff until you do.”
  • “They say they’re picking it up for you, but you’re not answering my phone calls. So I’m not giving it to them.”

It’s clear that she was the actor, and the one responsible for giving my stuff away. At no point did she say “Your friend came over and took your stuff,” or anything substantially equivalent. Nor did she use any language indicating that they barged in, broke in, climbed in through the window, or otherwise entered forcibly. Even the phrase “they’re picking it up for you” implies a handing over and acceptance, rather than an appropriation.

In a criminal case, the standard of proof is “beyond a reasonable doubt;” whereas in a civil case, which includes small claims, the standard of proof is “by the preponderance of evidence.” Or, as a lawyer told me, it’s more likely than not that the defendant did what the plaintiff alleges.

I think that any reasonable person would conclude that by the preponderance of evidence, it’s far more likely than not that she gave away my stuff as she already stated she did.

Facts aside, even if I intended on moving out that weekend which I didn’t, and I sent people to the apartment to pick up some of my stuff which I didn’t, I would have let her know. I would have told her who it was, when they were going to be there, and what they were going to pick up, because that would have been the considerate and reasonable thing to do.

Also, if I sent people to the apartment to pick up some of my stuff, it would have been for the purpose of them bringing it to me in which case I would still be in possession of my belongings and would have had no reason to sue her in small claims court.

And, if I sent people to the apartment to pick up some of my stuff, then there would likely be a record of this in my call and text message history of that night. However there’s no record of this because it never happened. She or anyone else can subpoena Google, Yahoo, Facebook, Instagram, Discord, Slack, Reverbnation, Metal-Archives, Discogs, Telegram, LinkedIn, X, MySpace, Friendster, or f***ing AOL and discover the same thing.

Lastly, if I were in her position and some complete strangers showed up at the apartment claiming that they were her friends and were there to pick up some of her stuff, I would have contacted her to confirm. If I couldn’t get a hold of her, I would have told the people to get her on the phone so that I could confirm with her myself. If they were unwilling or unable to do that, I would have said “Nope. Not happening,” and shut the door in their faces. I don’t care how rocky our relationship was at the time, I wouldn’t let complete strangers who show up in the middle of the night claiming to be her friends into the apartment to take her belongings without any confirmation from her.

Later that night at 11:23 PM when I was back at Leslie’s, she called and said she was going to put all my stuff on the porch and that I should come and get it. Over the following month while I was getting settled in at Leslie’s, I discovered some of her belongings mixed in with mine. I considered throwing it away, but I didn’t want to give her any excuse to accuse me of stealing her stuff, so I made several trips to return it.
